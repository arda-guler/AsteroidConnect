Basic Tutorial:
How do I identify asteroids?

Head to the "Data" page. In there, you will find three categories of data. Essentially, they are going to be very similar if you are new to this effort. I suggest ITF-ITF if you don't have any idea what to pick.

In that page, you will find astrometry charts and some text below them. The astrometry chart indicates the tracklets we are investigating:
- Yellow stars are a batch of tracklets that are likely to belong to the same object
- Red stars are batches of tracklets that might belong to the same object, observed between the oldest and latest yellow tracklets
- Blue stars are batches of tracklets that might belong to the same object, observed some time after the latest yellow tracket

These potential linkages were identified by the Connectonomicon software, but since the tolerances used were quite high, a lot of them are probably false positives. For the eventual decision, an orbit fit must be done.

You can use any software that can handle heliocentric orbits, including that of Connectonomicon itself, but Bill Gray's find_orb will be quite a lot of faster and it is usually more user-friendly.

The text files below the images are the astrometry data. The first lines before the empty lines are the yellow tracklets, the rest are blue and red tracklets.

The first 13 characters of each line is the designation of the minor planet. Take the astrometry data of the yellow tracklets, and insert them into the orbit determination software. If the RA-DEC residuals are low overall, you can work on these tracklets. Generally, to submit an object for identification, you need at least 3 distinct nights of observation for an object. If the yellow tracklets do not cover 3 distinct nights, try to match them with blue and yellow tracklets in the same data file, and attempt to fit orbits. If the orbit fit succeeds and the residuals are low, you can submit the identification to the MPC for final checking. To prepare your submission, read MPC's guidelines for submission formatting.
